Final Report Requirements

1. Complete Capstone Final Report (single PDF).

Your final report must include the following clearly labeled sections. Use headings and subheadings to
make the structure obvious and easy to follow.

• Introduction (Problem Framing and Motivation).

In the Introduction, frame your research
problem in a mathematical, statistical, or data science context. Make sure a reader can understand
what you study, why it matters, and what you plan to do. Include:

– Problem definition: Clearly articulate the research question or problem you are addressing. State

it in a precise way (define variables, quantities of interest, and what “success” means).

– Significance and motivation: Explain why this problem is important (scientific, societal, engi-

neering, business, or policy relevance). Clarify what gap your project addresses.

– Context and background: Summarize the relevant theories, models, or methods from math/statistics/data

science that your project builds on. Reference key ideas (e.g., regression, classification, time series,
optimization, stochastic modeling, clustering, inference). Include citations to credible sources.

– Objectives and goals: List the concrete objectives of your project. For example: predicting a
response, estimating parameters, identifying structure, comparing methods, or evaluating an inter-
vention/policy.

– Summary of approach: Provide a brief overview of your planned workflow (data → preprocessing
→ model/analysis → evaluation → interpretation). This should naturally lead into the Methods
section.

• Methods (Technical Approach and Reproducibility). The Methods section must explain exactly
what you did so another person could reproduce your analysis. Describe your pipeline with enough
detail to be replicable. Include:

– Data acquisition and sources:

• Results (What You Found). Present your finalized results clearly and honestly. A reader should

be able to see what you obtained and how strong the evidence is. Include:

– Core findings: Summarize the main numerical/empirical outcomes tied directly to your objectives.
– Visualizations and tables: Include well-labeled figures/tables (axes labels, units, legends, cap-

tions). Every figure/table must be referenced and explained in the text.

– Model performance and comparisons: Provide metrics on held-out data (or appropriate vali-

dation). Compare against baseline methods.

– Uncertainty and variability: When relevant, include confidence intervals, error bars, standard

deviations, or variability across folds/runs.

• Discussion (Meaning and Implications). Interpret what your results mean. This is where you

connect numbers back to the question and show critical thinking.

– Interpretation: Explain what the results imply about the research question. Highlight patterns,

mechanisms, or practical conclusions.

– Limitations and challenges: Discuss data limitations, modeling assumptions, potential confounds,

and sources of error.

– Implications: Explain how your work contributes (methodologically, scientifically, or practically).

If relevant, discuss how findings could be used in decision-making or real applications.

• Conclusion and Future Work (Wrap-up and Next Steps). Close the report with a clear summary

and realistic next directions.
